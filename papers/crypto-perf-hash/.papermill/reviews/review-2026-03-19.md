# Review: Cryptographic Perfect Hash Functions

**Paper:** Cryptographic perfect hash functions: A theoretical analysis on space efficiency and algebraic composition
**Author:** Alexander Towell
**Date:** 2026-03-19
**Reviewer:** Papermill (automated)

## Summary

The paper analyzes a theoretical perfect hash function constructed by exhaustive search over random oracle outputs. It derives a space bound of log2(e) - (1/r - 1)log2(1/(1-r)) bits/element (achieving 1.44 bits/element for r=1), proves a maximum entropy property, presents a two-level practical variant, and develops algebraic composition results for PHFs.

## Verdict: Promising but needs significant revision before submission

---

## 1. Mathematical Correctness

### 1.1. Space complexity derivation (Theorem 3.3)

**Issue: Conflation of E[log2 Q] and log2 E[Q].**

The proof computes E[Q] = 1/p(m,r), then claims "the expected bit length is given approximately by E[log2 Q] = log2(1/p(m,r))". This equates E[log2 Q] with log2(E[Q]), which is incorrect in general. By Jensen's inequality (log is concave), E[log2 Q] <= log2(E[Q]). For geometric random variables, the gap is nonzero.

The correct approach: For Q ~ Geometric(p), E[log2 Q] = -log2(p) + (correction terms involving the harmonic-like series). The paper gets the right answer by a different route (the bit-length PMF in the Appendix, Theorem A.3, gives E[N] = sum_{j=1}^{infty} q^{2^j-1}, which numerically matches the formula). But the proof of Theorem 3.3 as written is not rigorous---it takes log2 inside the expectation without justification.

**Severity: Medium.** The result is correct (confirmed by the independent PMF-based derivation in the Appendix), but the proof needs to be rewritten to avoid the invalid step.

### 1.2. Stirling's approximation step

The proof applies Stirling's approximation to log2(N!) and log2((N-m)!) and then simplifies. The intermediate algebra (lines 525-542) is correct: the m*log2(m/r) terms cancel properly, and the final simplification to log2(e) - (1/r - 1)log2(1/(1-r)) checks out. However, two issues:

- **Missing asymptotic qualifier.** The paper says "approximately" but does not specify the regime. Stirling's approximation requires N = m/r to be large. The paper should state that the result holds asymptotically as m -> infinity.

- **Line 526 notation.** The LHS says E[Q] but the RHS is a bit-length expression (bits). This is a notational error---it should say E[BL(n')] or E[log2 Q], not E[Q].

### 1.3. The 1.44 bits/element result (Theorem 3.6)

The limit computation is correct:
```
lim_{r->1} [log2(e) - (1/r - 1) log2(1/(1-r))]
= log2(e) - lim_{a->0} a log2(1/a)
= log2(e) - 0
= log2(e) ~ 1.44
```

This is standard and well-known. The result matches the information-theoretic lower bound for MPHFs.

### 1.4. Probability mass function (Theorem A.2)

The PMF p_N(n|m,r) = q^{2^n - 1}(1 - q^{2^n}) is correctly derived. The telescoping sum proof is clean and correct. The expected value derivation (Theorem A.3) via the telescoping trick is also correct.

### 1.5. Maximum entropy claim (Theorem 3.5)

**Issue: The claim that H(N) = E[BL(n')] is stated but not rigorously proved.**

The proof says the encoding is entropy-optimal because "the entropy of a geometric random variable equals the expected bit length." This is imprecise. The actual encoding is not of Q directly but of N = floor(log2 Q), and the bit-level encoding n' has a different distribution from Q. The paper needs to carefully distinguish:

- H(Q): entropy of the geometric random variable (in bits)
- H(N): entropy of the bit-length random variable N
- E[BL(n')]: expected bit length of the encoding

These are different quantities. The claim that the encoding achieves maximum entropy needs a more careful argument, specifically that each bit of the encoding n' is uniformly distributed and independent (which would follow from the random oracle assumption, but this needs to be made explicit).

**Severity: Medium.** The intuition is right but the proof is not rigorous.

### 1.6. Success probability (Theorem 3.2)

The derivation p(m,r) = P^N_m / N^m is standard birthday-problem combinatorics and is correct.

## 2. Algebraic Composition (Section 5)

### 2.1. Post-composition with injection (Theorem 5.1)

The theorem and proof are correct. If g is injective and h_A^r is a PHF, then g o h_A^r is a PHF with load factor r' = r/(1+alpha). The algebra in the proof checks out.

### 2.2. Permutation equivalence classes (Section 5.2)

The claim that |Y|! permutations generate a family of PHFs with identical collision structure is correct. The corollary about the ratio |Y|!/|Y|^|X| is straightforward.

### 2.3. Missing details

- **Pre-composition.** The paper mentions pre-composition with injective encoders (the paragraph after Theorem 3.1) but does not formalize it as a theorem. This should be stated formally to parallel the post-composition theorem.

- **Composition of two PHFs.** The paper does not address what happens when you compose two PHFs (e.g., h_B o h_A). This is relevant for the cipher map composability story and should be discussed.

- **No group/monoid structure.** The paper mentions "algebraic structure" and "equivalence classes" but does not identify the algebraic structure precisely. The set of PHFs under post-composition with injections/permutations forms a group action (the symmetric group acts on PHFs). Making this explicit would strengthen the contribution.

## 3. Novelty Assessment

### Known results

- The 1.44 bits/element lower bound for MPHFs is well established. It appears in various forms in Fredman and Komlos (1984), Mehlhorn (1982), and is standard textbook material.
- The birthday-problem probability P^N_m / N^m is standard.
- The geometric search construction is essentially the universal construction described by Fredman, Komlos, Szemeredi (1984), specialized to a random oracle.

### Potentially novel contributions

1. **The general load-factor formula.** The extension to arbitrary r, yielding log2(e) - (1/r - 1)log2(1/(1-r)), may be novel in this exact form, though the underlying analysis technique (geometric search over random functions) is standard.

2. **The maximum entropy claim.** The observation that the in-place encoding (the trial index) is a maximum entropy bit string is an interesting perspective, connecting PHF space bounds to source coding theory. However, the claim needs more rigorous formulation and proof (see above).

3. **The algebraic composition framework.** Formalizing post-composition with injections and permutation equivalence classes as an algebraic theory of PHFs appears to be a modest but useful contribution. However, the development is thin---it consists of one theorem and two examples.

4. **The two-level construction analysis.** The coupon-collector analysis of the two-level scheme is standard but the connection to the single-level space bound is nicely drawn.

### Comparison with known PHF space bounds

| Construction | Space (bits/element) | Time | Reference |
|-------------|---------------------|------|-----------|
| FKS two-level | O(n) total (not per-element optimal) | O(n) expected | Fredman et al. 1984 |
| CHD | ~2.07 (r=1) | O(n) | Belazzougui et al. 2009 |
| BPZ / FCH | ~3.6-4.0 | O(n) | Various |
| RecSplit | ~1.56 | O(n) amortized | Esposito et al. 2020 |
| SicHash | ~1.5 | O(n) | Lehmann & Sanders 2023 |
| This paper (theoretical) | 1.44 (r=1) | Exponential | -- |
| Information-theoretic LB | 1.44 | -- | Mehlhorn 1982 |

The paper achieves the lower bound but with exponential construction time. The novelty is in the analysis framework, not in a practical algorithm. **The paper should acknowledge more recent MPHF constructions** (RecSplit, SicHash, ShockHash) that achieve near-1.44 bits/element with practical construction times.

## 4. Structure and Completeness

### Issues

1. **Section 2 is too long.** The definitions of sets, Cartesian products, tuples, and bit strings (pp. 2-4) are standard material that could be condensed or moved to a preliminaries section. The paper spends about 3 pages defining basic mathematical concepts before getting to hash functions.

2. **Redundant text.** Lines 421-427 contain two paragraphs that both introduce Algorithm 1 with overlapping descriptions. One should be removed.

3. **Section 4 (two-level) is underspecified.** The two-level construction is presented via Algorithm 2 but the analysis is brief. The claim that "the expected space per bucket is log2(e) ~ 1.44 bits" (line 761) is not proved---it is stated as an observation. The coupon-collector analysis gives the expected number of *trials*, but converting this to expected *bits* requires the same geometric-to-bit-length argument from Section 3, which is not repeated here.

4. **Missing conclusion.** The paper ends abruptly after the Appendix. There is no conclusion or future work section summarizing the contributions and their significance.

5. **No explicit connection to cipher maps / trapdoor computing.** The paper mentions "oblivious sets and maps" in passing (line 573) but does not develop the connection to the broader trapdoor computing framework. This connection is important for the author's research program and should be made explicit.

6. **The Postulate (Postulate 3.1) is awkward.** The 1.44 bits/element lower bound for MPHFs is not a postulate---it is a theorem that can be proved information-theoretically. Calling it a postulate suggests it is assumed rather than derived, which is misleading. The paper should either prove it from first principles or cite the standard result and call it a theorem.

7. **Load factor definition inconsistency.** Definition 2.4 defines load factor as |hash_A(A)| / |image(hash_A)|, which is the ratio of *used* outputs to *total* outputs. But later the paper uses r = m/N = |A|/|Y|. These are the same only when h_A is surjective (which is assumed, Assumption 2.1), but this equivalence should be made explicit.

8. **Notation for N.** The paper uses N for both the codomain size (Section 3) and the random bit length (Appendix). This overloading is confusing.

### Missing material

- **No experimental validation.** The Mathematica notebooks exist in research/ but no numerical results are included in the paper beyond a single PMF plot. Numerical verification of the space bound for finite m would strengthen the claims.

- **No comparison plots.** A plot showing the space bound vs. load factor r would help readers understand the formula visually.

## 5. Relationship to the Bernoulli Hash Function Paper

The two papers share significant structural overlap:

### Shared elements
- The geometric search construction (exhaustive search over salts/trial indices)
- The PMF of the bit length: q^{2^n-1}(1-q^{2^n}) -- identical formula
- The expected bit length series: sum q^{2^j-1} -- identical
- The maximum entropy claim
- The telescoping sum proof technique

### Key differences
- **crypto-perf-hash** addresses perfect hashing (collision-free, r-parameterized)
- **bernoulli-hash-function** addresses approximate membership (epsilon-parameterized, with false positives/negatives)

### Recommendations
1. **Cross-reference explicitly.** Each paper should cite the other and explain the relationship clearly. The shared geometric search / PMF / entropy machinery should be acknowledged as common infrastructure.

2. **Factor out the shared theory.** The PMF derivation, telescoping sum, and expected bit length series appear identically in both papers. Consider extracting these into a shared lemma paper, or at minimum, one paper should cite the other for these results rather than re-deriving them.

3. **Unify the perspective.** The crypto-perf-hash paper's space bound log2(e) - (1/r-1)log2(1/(1-r)) and the BHF paper's space bound -log2(epsilon) are not competing results---they address different problems. Making this distinction crisp (exact membership vs. approximate membership) would help readers of both papers.

4. **The BHF paper is more mature.** The bernoulli-hash-function paper has a more developed discussion section, clearer proofs, and explicit comparison with practical data structures. The crypto-perf-hash paper would benefit from similar polish.

4. **Line 376:** "rated-distorted" -- undefined term. Remove or define.
5. **Line 512:** "m = floor(log2 n)" should be "BL(n') = floor(log2 n)" -- the variable m is already used for cardinality.
6. **Line 526:** E[Q] on the LHS should be E[BL(n')] or E[log2 Q] -- Q is the trial number, not the bit length.
7. **Line 573:** Citation "[phf,pmf]" -- \cite{phf} is Dietzfelbinger et al. (1990), which is about hash tables, not about "oblivious sets and maps." The citation seems misplaced.
8. **Line 747:** "p_j = (m-j+1)/m" -- this is the probability of *not* colliding with j-1 previously assigned hashes, which is correct only when codomain has exactly m slots (i.e., r=1). For general r, it should be (N-j+1)/N. This section seems to assume r=1 without stating it.
9. **Lines 769-770:** Var(T) formula appears to have m^2 in the wrong place. Each T_j ~ Geometric(p_j) has Var(T_j) = (1-p_j)/p_j^2. The sum should be sum_{j=2}^{m} (1-p_j)/p_j^2, not what is written.
